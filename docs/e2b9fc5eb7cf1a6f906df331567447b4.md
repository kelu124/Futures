## Summary

The article discusses the evolving landscape of theoretical physics, highlighting the shift from traditional mathematical modeling to the use of computer simulations. It notes the historical reluctance of some physicists to embrace numerical methods but emphasizes the growing acceptance of simulations in studying complex systems, such as galaxy formation and particle interactions. Furthermore, it explores the emergence of quantum simulations, where physicists create laboratory systems that mimic fundamental particles and interactions, leading to new insights in various areas, including quantum gravity and black hole behavior. The author poses questions about the nature of fundamental particles and the philosophy of scientific explanation, suggesting a potential future where experiments lead to predictions without relying heavily on mathematical models.
