## The Thirty-Five Day Window

Matt had never given helium much thought, which, he reflected, was exactly the problem with most things that kept civilisation running.^1

He worked in procurement for a mid-sized memory chip manufacturer in Icheon, sixty kilometres south of Seoul. His days were structured around spreadsheets that tracked noble gases the way a sommelier might track Burgundy vintages — by provenance, purity, and the increasingly troubling question of whether they’d arrive at all.

The Ras Laffan strike had changed everything. For three days, Matt’s phone had not stopped ringing. His counterpart in Doha, a quietly efficient woman named Avery, had gone silent. Her last message, sent before the facility’s communications went down, read simply: *Check your buffer stocks. I’m sorry.*

“Thirty-five to forty-eight days,” Matt said to his colleague Derek, tapping the container spec sheet. “That’s how long liquid helium stays viable in transit. After that, it warms up, turns to gas, and — this is the part I find philosophically devastating — leaves the atmosphere entirely.”

“Leaves the atmosphere,” Derek repeated.

“Escapes Earth. Forever. It’s the only industrial supply chain with an exit clause into space.”^2

Derek had his own problems. He’d been scrolling through a report showing that some American workers were now filming their daily chores and uploading personal phone calls to train AI models. The pay was negligible. The companies called it “micro-licensing.” Derek called it something else, but the office had rules about language before noon.

Their boss, Charlie, arrived late from a video call with Meta. She’d been briefed on Zuckerberg’s new personal agent — a system designed to flatten the management layers between him and his decisions. “He wants one agent per employee,” Charlie said, pouring herself a coffee with the deliberation of someone defusing a bomb. “The idea is that middle management becomes a kind of verification layer.”

“Doesn’t that make middle management... us?” Matt asked.

Charlie did not answer.^3

Over lunch, they discussed the World Bank’s reversal on industrial policy. For decades, the orthodoxy had been that governments shouldn’t pick winners. Now the Bank’s own chief economist was calling that stance as useful as a floppy disk. Matt, who had grown up in a country where industrial policy had built the semiconductor industry they all depended upon, found this belated acknowledgement slightly maddening, like being congratulated for knowing how to use a fork.

“The Americans deregulated their airlines the same way,” Derek offered. He’d been reading about how the Civil Aeronautics Board had been dismantled in 1978, turning a regulated public utility into an unregulated oligopoly. “They had a perfectly good system. They broke it on purpose. Now everybody’s legs are crammed into seventeen inches of space and the airports don’t serve half the country.”

“Sounds familiar,” Matt said, thinking of the helium.

That evening, walking home, Matt passed a group of children playing Pokémon Go. Thirty billion images, he’d read, crowdsourced from players like these — centimetre-accurate maps of the physical world, being used to train delivery robots. The children didn’t know they were building infrastructure. Neither did the helium.^4

He arrived home, opened his laptop, and checked the latest container shipment status. Thirty-one days in transit. Viable. Barely.

His daughter was watching a ninety-second AI-generated drama on her phone — something from a ByteDance vertical format factory. The plot, as far as he could tell, involved a betrayal, a revelation, and a perfectly timed zoom on a single tear. The whole thing was over before his kettle boiled.

“Was that good?” he asked.

She shrugged. “It’s something.”

Matt closed his laptop and sat in the kitchen, listening to the kettle’s whistle — a sound that, unlike helium, had the decency to stay in the room.
